08.SPREADSHEET SOUL

8.1 Administrative Existence

One of the most unusual pieces in Shadowbook is "Notes Towards a Spreadsheet Novel," which explores bureaucracy, administration, and the Excel spreadsheet as metaphors for contemporary existence. While seemingly distant from phones, this meditation reveals something about how digital technologies structure our consciousness.

The spreadsheet represents a particular way of organizing reality: everything in its cell, everything quantified, everything related through formulas. "We work in rows and columns. The rows are numbered and designate things. The columns have letters and mostly designate amounts."

The phone operates on similar logic. Our contacts are lists, our messages organized by time, our apps arranged in folders and home screens. The phone is a pocket-sized spreadsheet of our lives, organizing the chaos of experience into manageable, searchable, quantifiable units.

The essay begins with a definition: "spreadsheet (n.) 1965, from spread (n.) + sheet (n.)." This etymology reveals that the spreadsheet is older than we might think—it predates digital computers. But its full power emerged only with digitization, when the static paper spreadsheet became dynamic, when changing one cell could ripple through all others.

The phone similarly takes older forms and digitizes them. It's a phone, yes, but also a camera, a notebook, a calendar, a map, a library, a television, a radio, a game console. It takes all these discrete objects and combines them into one interface, one device, one organizing principle.

"The spreadsheet tells a story, a saga, a bureaucratic epic. We can assemble a morphology of the bureaucratic epic; it would be more simple than the morphology of the fairytale."

This bureaucratic epic is the story of contemporary life—the endless administration of the self, the constant input of data, the tracking and optimization and quantification. The phone is the primary interface for this self-administration, the device through which we perform the bureaucratic work of being a contemporary subject.

8.2 The Dream of Mutation

But Shadowbook finds something almost mystical in the spreadsheet's capacity for mutation: "As he changed an item in one cell, there was a ripple-like movement in the other cells; the spreadsheet program was recalculating... Then he punched in another variable, and another ripple of figures washed across the screen."

This ripple effect—change one thing and everything else changes—captures something about digital existence generally and phone use particularly. Every action we take on our phones has cascading effects: send one message and notifications ripple outward, post something and it propagates through networks, like a stone thrown in a pond creating expanding circles.

The essay describes this mutation in almost religious terms: "The ripple: that's the more. As in a dream, a dream dreaming of sex." The spreadsheet, seemingly the most boring and prosaic of digital technologies, reveals itself as containing something excessive, something beyond the merely functional.

The phone too contains this "more." It's not just a tool for communication or information—it's a portal to other worlds, other selves, other possibilities. The mutation it enables isn't just in data but in identity, in relationship, in understanding.

Shadowbook quotes from an early description of spreadsheet use: "'I can't begin to tell you how many hours I spend at this. This is my pet, in a way. Scratching its ears and brushing its code...it's almost an obsession.'" The spreadsheet, like the phone, becomes a pet, an obsession, something that consumes time and attention beyond any rational justification.

The essay develops a complex mythology around the spreadsheet, creating characters and themes: "The administrator," "The girls," "The bureaucrat," "The guru," "The project manager," "The invisible one." Each represents a different relationship to administrative technology, a different mode of being shaped by digital systems.

8.3 The Invisible Controller

The spreadsheet essay introduces "the invisible one" who "decides and yet isn't held responsible. He who thinks in bush structures." This figure might represent the algorithms that increasingly govern phone use: the recommendation engines, the ranking systems, the A/B tests that shape our experience without our awareness.

We think we control our phones, but increasingly they control us—or rather, the invisible systems operating through them do. They decide what we see, in what order, what notifications we receive, which connections are emphasized. The phone is both servant and master, responding to our commands while channeling our behavior in directions determined by entities we can't see.

The essay discusses "Evil": "Supposedly, 95% of all companies use Microsoft Excel. 1.2 billion people would have Office installed. The battle against evil is fought within the context of evil."

This description of the spreadsheet as evil—or at least as gray, ambiguous, power-laden—applies equally to the phone. The phone comes from companies whose business models depend on extracting and monetizing our attention and data. We carry devices that serve interests beyond our own, that work for corporations even as they work for us.

"To say the least, a spreadsheet is a 'gray medium'. A seemingly trivial outfit for an office clerk, the power of which nonetheless should not be underestimated." The phone too is gray—neither wholly good nor wholly bad, but powerful, shaping our lives in ways we don't fully understand or control.

The essay's final section discusses "The sublime": "Looking through your eyelashes a gray shadow can be seen rising up from the orderly patterns: it's a labyrinth made of perfect rectangles. As soon as you enter it, you see nothing, you just feel: first fear, then admiration, and finally sleep. Welcome to the 21st century sublime, Luna Park of evil, which you've entered without knowing and that has you lost."

The phone is also a labyrinth of perfect rectangles—its grid of apps, its organized interfaces, all leading us deeper into systems we don't understand. We're lost in our phones, wandering through endless content, endless connections, endless possibilities, unable to find our way out.

8.4 Life Measured in Spreadsheets

The connection between phones and spreadsheets becomes clearer when we consider how much of phone use involves inputting data, tracking metrics, administering the self. Health apps track our steps, our heart rate, our sleep. Finance apps track our spending. Productivity apps track our tasks. Social media provides metrics on our posts.

We've become our own administrators, clerks in the bureaucracy of the quantified self. The phone is the interface through which we perform this administrative labor, inputting data, checking metrics, adjusting behaviors based on numbers.

This self-administration is supposedly empowering—we're taking control of our lives, optimizing ourselves, becoming more efficient and effective. But it's also exhausting, alienating, reducing lived experience to data points and graphs.

Shadowbook's bureaucratic imagery captures this exhaustion: "You're being called upon by the state, so you can't really be innocent, you know that much, but still no one will let you in on the details whatsoever, basically you're a witness turning up late at your own crime scene."

We're witnesses to our own lives, observing ourselves through the phone's metrics and records, always slightly behind, never quite catching up to the present moment because we're too busy documenting it.
