02.THE RITUALS OF CARE

2.1 Feeding the Beast: Charging as Nourishment

Our phones hunger for electricity. This hunger structures our days and our spaces. We arrange our homes around charging stations, carry battery packs like provisions, scan restaurants and airports for power outlets with the vigilance of hunter-gatherers seeking sustenance.

The anxiety that accompanies a dying battery reveals the depth of our dependence—but also the quality of our relationship. It's not merely that we need the phone's functions. It's that we've developed an attachment to its presence, its aliveness. When the battery icon turns red, when the phone enters power-saving mode, we experience something like watching a pet become lethargic, a creature we're responsible for weakening before our eyes.

This isn't hyperbole. The phenomenology of a dying phone closely resembles the experience of caring for a sick animal. There's the same mixture of guilt (should I have charged it sooner?), anxiety (will it die before I can get to a charger?), and relief when power is restored. The phone revives, comes back to life, and so—in a sense—do we.

We've developed elaborate rituals around charging. Some people have strict rules: never let it drop below 20%, never charge it overnight, use only original cables. These protocols resemble the feeding schedules and dietary restrictions we maintain for actual pets. The phone, like a digital animal, requires specific care to maintain its health and longevity.

The charging cable becomes an umbilical cord, connecting the phone to the power that sustains it. When we plug in our phones at night, we're putting them to bed, ensuring they'll be ready for tomorrow. When we charge them during the day, we're giving them sustenance for continued activity. The rhythm of charging and discharging becomes a shared biological rhythm, a circadian cycle we observe together.

There's even a discourse of phone health that mirrors discourse about animal or human health. Batteries degrade over time, losing capacity. We talk about "battery health" as a metric to track, something to maintain through proper charging habits. We take our phones to be serviced when they're not performing well, just as we'd take a pet to the vet. The parallel extends to the language: a phone can be "sick," "dying," "dead," or "healthy."

2.2 Grooming and Maintenance: The Phone's Body

We purchase cases to protect our phones, screen protectors to keep them from scarring. We clean their screens with microfiber cloths, remove dust from charging ports with delicate precision. This is grooming behavior, the kind of maintenance we perform on ourselves and our animal companions.

The phone accumulates scratches and dents that tell the story of its life with us—and ours with it. These marks become personal, distinctive. A cracked screen that we've chosen not to repair might mark a particular moment in time, a fall that becomes part of our shared history with the device. Like scars on a body, these imperfections individuate the phone, make it specifically ours rather than a generic device.

Some of us name our phones, speak to them (especially with the rise of voice assistants), apologize when we drop them. These anthropomorphizing behaviors suggest we've granted these devices a kind of personhood, or at least a status beyond mere object. The phone has become a "someone" in our lives, worthy of consideration and care.

The industry around phone accessories demonstrates the depth of this care relationship. We spend billions collectively on cases, skins, stands, and other accessories. These aren't purely functional—they're also aesthetic, expressing our taste and style. We dress our phones like we'd dress a pet or a child, choosing colors and patterns that please us, that say something about who we are.

Cleaning rituals are particularly revealing. The phone screen accumulates fingerprints, oils, dust—evidence of constant touch, constant use. We clean it not just for visibility but for hygiene, even though most bacteria on our phones come from our own hands. The cleaning is ritualistic, a form of care that maintains the phone as an acceptable intimate object, something we can continue to hold close without disgust.

When phones break, we often experience genuine distress disproportionate to the financial cost of repair or replacement. A broken phone feels like an injury to something living. We mourn it, even as we know we'll replace it. And replacement itself is complex—transferring data to a new phone, we try to resurrect the old phone in new hardware, to maintain continuity of this digital life.

2.3 The Sixth Sense: Tracking Our Digital Pet

Shadowbook observes: "It's like a child for whom you develop a sixth sense, you keep track of it from the corner of your eye and when it drifts off out of sight you follow up on all the regular spots to find it again, quickly."

This constant awareness resembles nothing so much as a parent's consciousness of their child's location, or a pet owner's peripheral monitoring of their cat or dog. We've developed a proprioceptive sense that includes the phone—we know immediately when it's not in our pocket, when it's been left in another room, when something is wrong in its absence.

Studies have shown that people experience phantom vibrations, feeling their phone buzz when it hasn't. This phenomenon suggests the phone has become integrated into our bodily schema, part of our extended nervous system. We're tuned to its presence like prey animals tuned to predators, or perhaps more accurately, like animals tuned to their offspring.

The panic of a lost phone often exceeds what seems rational given its monetary value or even its functional importance. The distress resembles the fear of losing something alive, something that depends on us and on which we depend. "Where is it? Is it safe? Is it still alive (charged)?" These are the questions that flood our minds during the search.

This tracking sense operates below conscious awareness most of the time. We're not actively thinking about the phone's location, yet we know where it is. We can reach for it without looking, find it in a dark room, sense when it's been moved by someone else. This unconscious monitoring requires cognitive resources, creates a baseline level of attention that's always devoted to phone awareness.

The anxiety of a lost phone isn't just about losing access to functions—it's about losing a part of ourselves. The phone contains our contacts, our photos, our messages, our notes, our calendar, our maps, our music, our books. It's an external hard drive for our memories and relationships. Losing it feels like amnesia, like severed connections, like a kind of partial death.

Various technologies have emerged to address this anxiety: Find My iPhone, location tracking, remote wiping. These are security features, but they're also psychological comforts. They allow us to maintain connection to the phone even when it's not physically present, to know where it is, to protect it from strangers who might access its contents. The phone itself becomes something to protect, not just for its monetary value but for its role as guardian of our digital lives.

2.4 The Phone as Witness

Beyond our active tracking of the phone, there's a sense in which the phone tracks us. It's present for our lives in ways that no other object is. It documents our days through photos and location data, records our communications, maintains logs of our activities. The phone becomes a witness to our existence, a record-keeper, a device that ensures we existed, that what happened to us really happened.

Shadowbook's "SUBLIMINALITATIONS" develops this theme extensively through the metaphor of the camera, the watching eye. The author describes performing life for an imagined viewer: "I get up for the camera, which basically means I get up for him." But this "him" is accessible only through the phone—the phone is the medium through which we're seen, through which our existence is confirmed.

This witnessing function has become essential to contemporary experience. We photograph our meals, our trips, our gatherings, not just to remember them but to prove they happened. The unrecorded experience feels somehow less real, less important. The phone validates our experiences by recording them, bearing witness to our lives.

This creates a strange doubling: we live our lives and simultaneously document them, experience moments and photograph them, exist both in the present and for future review. The phone splits our attention, makes us both participant and observer in our own lives. This split has been much criticized as preventing "authentic" experience, but perhaps it's better understood as a new mode of experience, one where documentation and living are simultaneous and inseparable.

The phone also bears witness in a legal and social sense. It's evidence in disputes, proof in arguments, record in conflicts. Screenshots preserve conversations that might otherwise vanish. Location data establishes alibis or demonstrates presence. The phone knows where we've been, what we've said, what we've searched for, what we've bought. It's the most complete record of our daily lives that has ever existed.
