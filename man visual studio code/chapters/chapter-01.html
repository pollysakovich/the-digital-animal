01.THE ANIMAL AWAKENS

1.1Introduction: What We Carry

We clutch them in our palms, press them against our cheeks, nestle them in our pockets close to our bodies. Our smartphones have become something more than tools—they have transformed into companions, extensions of ourselves, digital creatures that demand feeding, attention, and constant care. As one writer observed in Shadowbook, the phone becomes "an electric animal that you turn about in your hand, just to feel its contours and the possibilities that are contained within it."

This exploration examines the metamorphosis of the smartphone from mere device to living presence—what we might call the "digital animal." Drawing on philosophical inquiry, personal observation, and literary reflection from Miriam Rasch's Shadowbook: Writing Through the Digital 2014-2018, we'll investigate how these objects have achieved a peculiar kind of life in our hands and minds, how they've become our most intimate companions, and what this relationship reveals about contemporary existence.

The term "digital animal" isn't merely metaphorical. It describes a genuine phenomenological experience—the way we relate to our phones resembles nothing so much as our relationship with pets, with living creatures that depend on us and on which we depend. This comparison might seem frivolous at first, but examining it seriously reveals profound truths about how technology has reshaped human existence in the twenty-first century.

1.2The First Recognition: Kornél Esti's Morning Companion

The recognition that our technologies might be alive—or at least alive to us—predates the smartphone by decades. In Shadowbook, there's a striking reference to Hungarian writer Dezső Kosztolányi's character Kornél Esti, who "in the morning when he woke up... had the telephone brought to him in bed. He put it by his pillow, under his warm quilt, like other people put the cat. He liked that electric animal."

This image from the interbellum period captures something essential: the telephone as pet, as warm-blooded presence despite its electrical coldness. The comparison to a cat is particularly apt. Cats choose us as much as we choose them. They remain mysterious, independent, yet somehow needful of our proximity. They purr with electricity—or is it that our phones vibrate with a kind of digital purring?

The cat is an especially resonant comparison because cats have always occupied an ambiguous position in human society. Unlike dogs, bred for millennia to serve human purposes, cats domesticated themselves, choosing to live alongside humans while maintaining their independence. They're simultaneously wild and tame, intimate and distant, present and aloof. The smartphone shares these qualities—it's ours, yet it connects to vast networks we don't control. It's in our hands, yet it serves interests beyond our own.

Kosztolányi's vision was prophetic. He saw in the landline telephone something that would become exponentially more true of its descendant, the smartphone. The phone is the animal that succeeded in domesticating humans even as we thought we were domesticating it. It's the pet that owns us.

The smartphone has intensified this relationship exponentially. Unlike Esti's landline, which stayed tethered to walls and tables, our devices follow us everywhere. They sleep beside us, wake us, accompany us to the bathroom, sit with us at meals. As noted in Shadowbook, "it lies in bed on the pillow besides you, it nestles in your pocket, ready to vibrate, right next to the loins."

This physical intimacy matters. The phone occupies spaces traditionally reserved for lovers, for children, for our most trusted companions. It knows our routines better than we do. It tracks our movements, our communications, our preferences, our secrets. It's present for our most private moments.

1.3Embodiment: The Phone as Fleshy Portal

The smartphone occupies a strange category between the mechanical and the organic. Its glass and metal construction speaks of industry and manufacture, yet the way we interact with it—through touch, through our fingertips reading its warm screen—creates an intimacy typically reserved for living things.

One theorist quoted in Shadowbook describes the smartphone as "still deeply part of a network of blood; an embodied, intimate, fleshy portal that penetrates into one's mind, into endless information, into other people." This characterization refuses the traditional separation between technology and biology. The phone becomes a kind of prosthetic organ, an extension of our nervous system that connects us to a vast body of data and human connection.

Consider the phenomenology of phone use: we stroke it, we caress it, we hold it close to our faces. We speak into it and listen through it. We feed it power through charging cables, become anxious when its battery life drains. These are not the behaviors we have toward mere tools. A hammer returns to the toolbox and is forgotten. The phone must be visible, accessible, alive in our awareness at all times.

The "fleshy portal" description captures something crucial—the phone is a membrane between states, between worlds. It's the boundary where the digital and physical meet, where information becomes tactile, where distant others become present. When we touch our screens, we're touching—through layers of mediation—other people, other places, other times. The phone makes the intangible tangible, the absent present.

This embodied relationship has changed how we understand bodies themselves. The phone has become part of our body schema—the unconscious map we maintain of our physical self. Studies show that people's reaction times slow when their phones are taken away, as if they've lost a limb. We experience phantom vibrations, feeling the phone buzz when it hasn't, because we've incorporated it into our somatic awareness.

The warmth of the phone matters too. It heats up with use, becoming literally warm in our hands. This warmth mimics the warmth of living flesh, creating a bodily feedback loop that reinforces the sense that we're holding something alive. When the phone is cold—turned off, dead—it feels wrong, uncanny, like touching a corpse.

1.4The Phone as Companion Species

Donna Haraway coined the term "companion species" to describe the co-evolutionary relationship between humans and dogs. They've shaped each other over millennia, each becoming what they are through relationship with the other. The smartphone represents a new kind of companion species—one that evolves not over millennia but over years, constantly updating, constantly changing, yet always remaining somehow the same.

We're in the early stages of this co-evolution, but already we can see how phones are shaping us. Our attention spans, our communication styles, our sense of privacy, our relationship to memory, our experience of time and space—all have been transformed by these devices. And we in turn shape them, teaching algorithms what we want, training AI assistants, developing new uses and practices that feed back into design.

This mutual shaping means we can't simply "put down our phones" without fundamentally changing who we are. The phone isn't an accessory we can remove; it's become constitutive of contemporary human being. This isn't necessarily dystopian—all tools shape their users. The question is whether we're shaping this relationship consciously or drifting into it unawares.

1.5Against Nostalgia: The Phone as Continuation

It's tempting to view the smartphone as a rupture, a break from more "natural" or "authentic" human existence. But Shadowbook resists this nostalgic view. As Rasch notes in discussing the post-digital condition, we're not in a world where the digital is new or separate—we're in a world where digital and analog have become inseparable.

Humans have always extended themselves through technology. Writing was once feared as a destroyer of memory. The printing press was thought to threaten social order. The telephone was criticized for its intrusion into domestic space. Each technology generated anxiety about loss of authenticity, loss of human connection, loss of some essential humanness.

The phone continues this long history. It's just the latest in a series of prosthetics, extensions, supplements to human capacity. What makes it distinctive is its density—it combines so many functions, mediates so many aspects of life, that it becomes not just a tool for specific purposes but an environment we inhabit.

Recognizing this continuity doesn't mean uncritical acceptance. It means understanding that our relationship with phones isn't unprecedented, that we can draw on long traditions of thinking about technology and human being, that the questions we face—about attention, connection, authenticity, agency—are variations on ancient themes.
